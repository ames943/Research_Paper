## 1. nc=4 Trajectory Anomaly

### Observed anomaly

In the main grid (method=none, npc=40, sig=0.027, bat=0.08), uncorrected AUC follows:

| nc | n_total | mean AUC | SD   | SEM  |
|----|---------|----------|------|------|
| 1  | 40      | 0.544    | 0.163 | 0.052 |
| 2  | 80      | 0.463    | 0.076 | 0.024 |
| 3  | 120     | 0.452    | 0.046 | 0.015 |
| 4  | 160     | 0.520    | 0.067 | 0.021 |

The nc=4 "recovery" (0.452 → 0.520, diff = +0.067) is **outside 1 SD of nc=3**
(nc3_SD = 0.046, nc4_SD = 0.067; |diff| > both SDs).

Informal t-statistic (pooled SEM of two 10-rep means):
`t = +0.0675 / sqrt((0.046² + 0.067²)/10) = +2.63`

By this criterion the recovery is **not pure noise** — but the key confound is
that nc=4 has n_total=160 vs nc=3 n_total=120: **33% more samples**. The question
is whether the recovery is caused by adding a 4th cohort or simply by having
more data.

### Supplementary sweep: fixed n_total=120

To disentangle "more cohorts" from "more data", a supplementary sweep was run
with n_total fixed at 120 (nc=1→npc=120, nc=2→npc=60, nc=3→npc=40, nc=4→npc=30),
30 replicates per cell (vs 10 in the main grid).

**method=none (uncorrected), n_total=120:**

| nc | npc | mean AUC | SD   | SEM  |
|----|-----|----------|------|------|
| 1  | 120 | 0.500    | 0.061 | 0.011 |
| 2  | 60  | 0.519    | 0.074 | 0.014 |
| 3  | 40  | 0.477    | 0.078 | 0.014 |
| 4  | 30  | 0.491    | 0.077 | 0.014 |

**AUC is NOT monotonically decreasing with nc when n_total is fixed.**
The nc=2 cell (npc=60) actually shows the *highest* AUC, and nc=4 (npc=30) sits
between nc=1 and nc=3. All four values are within ~1 SEM of each other —
the spread (0.477–0.519) is explained by sampling variance at this effect size.

All corrections in the fixed-n_total sweep (nc=3, npc=40):

| method           | AUC   | SD    |
|------------------|-------|-------|
| none             | 0.477 | 0.078 |
| mean_centering   | 0.498 | 0.081 |
| location_scale   | 0.493 | 0.079 |
| quantile_mapping | 0.518 | 0.061 |
| cohort_covariate | 0.484 | 0.081 |

### Conclusion: nc=4 anomaly

The recovery is a **sample-size artifact**. When n_total is held constant at 120:
- AUC is non-monotonic across nc (range 0.477–0.519, all within ≤1.4 SEM of each other).
- There is no evidence of monotonic degradation from adding more cohorts when
  per-cohort n decreases proportionally. All configurations at this signal strength
  produce AUC indistinguishable from chance (0.5) within noise.
- The observed nc=4 recovery in the main grid (n=160) is explained by the additional
  40 samples compensating for the added batch heterogeneity.

**Paper implication:** The real-data trajectory (nc=1→2→3 at roughly fixed npc≈39–40)
is the correct comparison for the paper — n_total grows alongside nc in that setting too.
The simulation confirms the trajectory (0.544→0.463→0.452) is a real signal-dilution
effect: adding heterogeneous cohorts without proportionally increasing signal hurts.
Do not claim "AUC worsens monotonically with nc at fixed n_total" — the fixed-n_total
sweep does not support this. The correct framing is: "pooling heterogeneous cohorts
without batch correction degrades performance in the real-data trajectory, where n_total
grows with nc but per-cohort n stays fixed at ≈39–40."

## 2. Per-cell SD for the Quantile Mapping "64% of cells" / "mean Δ=+0.04" Claims

### Per-cell SD by method (nc≥2, sig_f2≥0.027, 108 cells each)

| method           | n_cells | SD p25 | SD p50 | SD p75 | mean Δ AUC | med SEM | Δ/med_SEM |
|------------------|---------|--------|--------|--------|------------|---------|-----------|
| none             | 108     | 0.045  | 0.063  | 0.082  | −0.007     | 0.020   | −0.36     |
| mean_centering   | 108     | 0.038  | 0.054  | 0.080  | +0.003     | 0.017   | +0.18     |
| location_scale   | 108     | 0.042  | 0.063  | 0.084  | −0.006     | 0.020   | −0.29     |
| quantile_mapping | 108     | 0.039  | 0.063  | 0.093  | **+0.042** | 0.020   | **+2.13** |
| cohort_covariate | 108     | 0.045  | 0.059  | 0.075  | −0.002     | 0.019   | −0.09     |

Δ AUC is measured vs the single-cohort no-batch ceiling.

### Is the quantile_mapping advantage distinguishable from noise?

**Yes, but with important nuance.**

- **Mean Δ / median SEM = 2.13.** On average, the per-cell effect is 2.1× the
  per-cell SEM. This is above the noise floor: the average cell is producing a
  positive estimate with signal-to-noise above 1.

- **Mean |t| = 2.86**, fraction of cells with |t|>1: 69%. Most individual cells
  have point estimates exceeding their own SEM.

- **Fraction Δ > 0: 86%** (not 64% — the earlier "64%" used the stricter threshold
  Δ > +0.02; the basic positive-fraction is 86%).

- Under a naive binomial test (H₀: each cell independently 50/50 positive):
  z ≈ 7.5, p ≪ 0.001 for 86/108 cells. However, cells sharing the same sig_f2
  level are NOT independent — all high-signal cells (sig=0.10) are positive by
  construction, and cells within the same (nc, npc) block are correlated.

### Source of the quantile_mapping advantage

The fixed-n_total supplementary sweep reveals that quantile_mapping also
outperforms "none" in the nc=1 (npc=120) condition:

| nc | npc | qmap AUC | none AUC | diff |
|----|-----|----------|----------|------|
| 1  | 120 | 0.514    | 0.500    | +0.014 |
| 2  | 60  | 0.485    | 0.519    | **−0.034** |
| 3  | 40  | 0.518    | 0.477    | +0.041 |
| 4  | 30  | 0.502    | 0.491    | +0.011 |

**Key observation:** at nc=2 (npc=60), quantile_mapping is 0.034 *below* "none".
The advantage is not consistent across all nc/npc combinations — it depends on
the ratio of per-cohort n to per-feature (the ECDF quality is better with more
training samples per batch). With npc=60 per cohort in training folds (~48
training samples per batch), the ECDF-to-reference mapping appears to
over-correct relative to npc=40 or npc=120.

This suggests the quantile_mapping advantage in the main grid partly reflects
specific npc/nc configurations rather than a universally robust correction.

### Claims that need softening

| Original claim | Revised framing |
|----------------|-----------------|
| "64% of cells positive for quantile_mapping" | "86% of cells show Δ > 0; 64% exceed +0.02 AUC" — the basic fraction is higher |
| "mean Δ = +0.04 for quantile_mapping" | Accurate. Δ/SEM = 2.13 — above per-cell noise, but: (a) cells are not independent; (b) at nc=2/npc=60 the method HURTS (−0.034); (c) all absolute AUCs remain near chance at this signal level |
| "quantile_mapping: consistent advantage across 64% of cells" | More accurate: "quantile_mapping shows the most consistently positive Δ AUC among standard methods (86% positive, mean Δ=+0.04, Δ/SEM=2.1), but the advantage is configuration-dependent — it reverses at some npc/nc combinations and disappears at nc=1 (single-cohort, no batch to correct)" |
| Implicit claim: "quantile_mapping should be recommended for the real data" | Not supported. The advantage is real in the simulation's consistent-signal model; but the fixed-n_total sweep shows reversal at nc=2/npc=60, and Phase 0.5's cross-cohort transfer failures suggest the real signal is cohort-heterogeneous, where this advantage would likely not replicate |

### Summary: is "+0.04 meaningful or within noise"?

It is **above per-cell noise** (Δ/SEM = 2.13), which means the positive direction
is not a coin flip across the full 108-cell regime. However:

1. **Absolute scale**: +0.04 AUC at a baseline of ~0.47–0.52 is unlikely to be
   detectable in a real study at n=120. It does not change the qualitative conclusion
   (all methods near chance at the real-data operating point).

2. **Configuration dependence**: the advantage reverses at nc=2/npc=60 (fixed-n_total
   sweep). The sign of the benefit depends on npc/nc specifics.

3. **Consistent-signal artifact**: the simulation injects identical response shifts
   in all cohorts. If the real signal is cohort-heterogeneous (as Phase 0.5 implies),
   this advantage would shrink or disappear.

4. **Other methods**: mean_centering (+0.003, Δ/SEM=+0.18), location_scale (−0.006,
   Δ/SEM=−0.29), and cohort_covariate (−0.002, Δ/SEM=−0.09) are all indistinguishable
   from zero — only quantile_mapping shows a clear positive signal.

**Paper framing recommendation:** Report quantile_mapping as showing the most
consistent positive direction (Δ/SEM≈2, 86% positive) among the four standard
methods, but note that (a) the absolute gain is small (+0.04 AUC), (b) all methods
remain near chance at the real-data signal level, (c) the advantage is
configuration-dependent and disappears at nc=2 in fixed-n_total analysis, and
(d) the simulation assumes a consistent-signal model that the real Phase 0.5 results
contradict. The appropriate sentence in the paper is:
"Quantile mapping showed the most consistently positive direction among standard
methods (mean ΔAUC = +0.04 vs ceiling, Δ/SEM ≈ 2.1 across the multi-cohort
non-null regime), though the absolute gain was small relative to baseline AUC
uncertainty and did not persist consistently when n_total was held fixed."
